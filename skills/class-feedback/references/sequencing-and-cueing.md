# Sequencing and cueing audit

The craft rubric behind the self-review mode. Use it to make a critique specific
and mechanical instead of vibes-based. Every finding here should name a *where*
and a *why*, and pair with a fix.

## The arc

A well-built class moves through a shape:

```
heat  →  prep  →  express  →  stabilize  →  integrate  →  down-regulate
```

Name where the arc breaks. Common breaks:

- **Peak arrives cold.** The express phase (peak pose or hardest work) lands
  before the prep trained its ingredients. Fix: trace the peak back to its parts
  and check each got a rehearsal earlier.
- **No down-regulation.** Class ends hot — straight from the peak to savasana
  with no stabilize/integrate. Students leave wired. Fix: earn the rest.
- **Front-loaded prep.** So long a warm-up that the express phase gets rushed.
  Fix: cut prep that doesn't feed the peak.

## Sequencing audit — ask these

1. **Did the warm-up earn the peak?** Every prep should train an ingredient the
   peak consumes. A peak that needs open hips, a rooted stance, and shoulder
   external rotation should have rehearsed all three. Flag preps that pay off
   nothing and peaks that arrive cold.
2. **Was anything unsafe or under-prepped?** General flags only: a deep backbend
   with no thoracic or hip-flexor prep, a fast load on cold joints, a strong
   inversion with no build. Do **not** invent injuries, contraindications, or
   medical advice — flag the structural gap, not a diagnosis.
3. **Did repetition serve a purpose?** A repeated round should teach something
   (add a layer, refine an action, build heat toward a target) or it is padding
   the clock. Ask which it was.
4. **Both sides.** Note where the second side diverged from the first and whether
   that was intentional (a taught refinement) or drift (ran out of time, forgot
   a cue).
5. **Was the cool-down enough to down-regulate?** Count the real minutes of
   down-shift against the intensity of the peak.

## Cueing audit — ask these

1. **Verb + body part + direction?** Strong cues are actionable: "press the
   right heel down and spin the inner thigh back." Weak cues are vague: "find
   your foundation," "be present in the pose." Flag the vague ones with the spot
   they happened.
2. **Too many words?** Count cues per pose. If students are holding while the
   teacher stacks a fourth and fifth cue, the instruction is burying the action.
3. **Was the theme taught or just announced?** A theme stated in the opening and
   never referenced again was announced. A theme woven into cues ("let this be
   the letting-go we talked about") was taught. Say which happened.
4. **Timing.** Was the cue given before the movement (so students can act on it)
   or after (so it's a correction)? Prospective cues teach; retrospective cues
   scold.

## Turning the audit into "the one change"

A self-review ends with a single change to make next time. Pick it by impact:
the one fix that would most improve the class, stated as a concrete action, tied
to the pattern that supports it. Not "work on pacing" but "cut the second sun-B
round and use those three minutes to prep the hips before pigeon." Everything
else in the audit is context for that one change and the one or two things to
**keep** untouched.
